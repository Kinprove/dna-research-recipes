# Recipe: Work an unknown-parentage case with DNA matches and WATO

## Goal

Place an unknown person — an unknown parent, adoptee's bio-parent, or an unplaced ancestor — into a tree, using the cM they share with known DNA matches. **WATO ("What Are The Odds?", a DNA Painter tool built by Leah Larkin (the DNA Geek) and Jonny Perl, with the underlying probability calculations derived by Leah Larkin and Andrew Millard) is the odds engine for that placement:** you build the matches' shared tree, propose candidate slots, and it scores each slot against relationship probability distributions. This recipe is the *judgment* around that engine — what a human must decide, what the tool computes, and where the whole thing quietly breaks.

**Where the probabilities come from (get the source right):** WATO's odds are derived from **AncestryDNA-based simulations** (Larkin & Millard), *not* from the crowd-sourced Shared cM Project. Blaine Bettinger's Shared cM Project data powers a **separate** DNA Painter tool — the "Shared cM Tool" — which you use to turn a cM figure into a *range of candidate relationships*. Two different tools, two different data sources; don't conflate them.

## The mental model (get this right or everything downstream breaks)

WATO does **not** find your ancestor. It **ranks where an unknown person could attach to a tree you have already built**, by scoring the observed match-cM against relationship probability distributions. Two facts follow, and skipping either produces confident garbage:

1. **The output is a set of competing hypotheses with *relative* odds — never a verdict.** A single hypothesis you enter arbitrarily proves nothing — the signal is the *ratio* between the candidates, so a "22%" or "score of 8×" only means something against the alternatives on the same tree. (The exception DNA Painter documents: when an *exhaustive* hypothesis set collapses to one surviving feasible slot, that convergence is a genuinely strong result — but it's the exhaustiveness that earns it, not a lone entry.) (Family Locket, WATO Plus, 2025-03-27)
2. **The math is only valid if each match has exactly one relationship path to the target, and the tree's founding couple is correct.** WATO assumes single-path descent. Pedigree collapse / endogamy / a match related through two lines silently invalidates the score — the tool has no way to know and will still rank confidently. (Family Locket, 2025-03-27)

The reframe that keeps you sane: **DNA is a tool that complements your existing research skills, not a separate discipline** — genetic networks sort the matches; targeted DNA testing and/or documents close the case. (Diahan Southard, Your DNA Guide, 2025-11-19)

## The reasoning arc — the human-owned sequence

This is the spine. WATO only enters at step 4; everything before and after is human judgment.

1. **Cluster the target's matches into ancestral groups.** Use genetic networks / shared-matches (ICW) / a Leeds-style sort to separate which line each match comes from before you trust any of them. A DNA network graph surfaces the *cluster* of related matches; identifying the cluster's likely most-recent-common-ancestor still takes documentary/tree research on top of it. (Southard 2025-11-19; Family Locket RLP 387, 2025-12-08)
2. **Identify the common ancestral couple of the strongest cluster** — the MRCA the tree will hang from. A useful *heuristic*: two strong matches who share DNA with the target but **not with each other** often descend from two different lines that meet at the target, so their intersection point is a candidate placement. Treat this as a lead, not a law — the two matches could instead sit on *opposite parental sides*, or be genuinely distant on a single line, in which case they never converge on the target. Verify with the tree and additional matches before betting on the intersection. (Kitty Cooper, 2026-01-09)
3. **Build the known descendancy tree** from that couple down to the present, placing each real DNA match at its documented position. GEDCOM import builds this fast (limit imported candidates to descendants of the selected ancestor). Model half- vs full-sibling relationships correctly — a wrong half/full edge skews every probability downstream. (Family Locket, 2025-03-27)
4. **Place hypotheses** — every plausible open slot where the target could descend — and enter the shared cM between the target and each match already in the tree. WATO scores each slot.
5. **Read the ratios and iterate — do not read a single run as an answer.** When a large or high-variance tree floods WATO with hypotheses and buries the strong branch, **prune to the branch carrying the strongest evidence and re-run — don't bulk up the tree.** (Family Locket, 2025-03-27; a case-specific tactic reported by the cited source, not independently verified.)
6. **Take the top hypothesis forward.** WATO ends the *scoring* step, not the investigation — its output points to the next move, which may be **more targeted DNA testing** (recruit a specific relative to test) *or* **documentary research**, usually both (see the final sections).

## What the tool (and an AI) can carry

- **The odds arithmetic itself** — WATO's whole reason to exist. Never compute cM→relationship odds by hand or by asking an LLM (it fabricates them); route it to **WATO** (for slot odds, given a tree + multiple matches) or **DNA Painter's Shared cM Tool / the Shared cM Project** (to turn a *single* cM figure into the *range* of candidate relationships). Those are two different tools with two different outputs — don't ask one to do the other's job.
- **Mechanical prep an AI is genuinely good at:** turning a match list or screenshots into a clean cM/relationship table; drafting a timeline from census rows; restructuring a GEDCOM subtree into a candidate list; drafting proof-argument *prose* for you to verify.
- **Surfacing candidate intersections** — e.g. duplicate-checking in tree software as a shortcut to find where two floating branches *may* meet (a lead to verify, not a proven junction). (Kitty Cooper, 2026-01-09)
- **Bookkeeping that saves re-work:** record each match's initials + cM in a systematic slot — Kitty Cooper uses the tree's *Suffix* field (e.g. a match's initials plus its size in asterisks) so it never collides with ThruLines/Hints. (Kitty Cooper, 2026-01-09)

## What to never trust or delegate (the judgment a human must own)

- **A match with multiple relationship paths / endogamy / pedigree collapse.** This is the #1 WATO-killer. Such matches share *inflated* cM and violate the single-path assumption — exclude them from hypothesis generation, don't feed them in "to be safe." In a documented case, a match was pulled from the initial run precisely because he had a possible *second* shared line. (Family Locket, 2025-03-27)
- **Total shared cM is not proof of a shared *ancestor*.** A total-cM figure can reflect one genuinely shared ancestral segment *or* several unrelated small segments that happen to sum to the same number — WATO scores the total either way, so don't over-trust a placement whose total leans on small fragments. What corroborates a real shared line is a **triangulated segment group** — three or more people who all match *one another* (not merely you) on the same segment — tracked via **chromosome mapping** (DNA Painter's companion to WATO). Two limits keep it honest: triangulation needs *mutual* pairwise matching among all members (not just overlap of the same region relative to the target), and even a clean group evidences a common *source*, not a specific *couple* — that attribution still needs documents.
- **A cM-based "estimated relationship" as fact.** A tool's predicted relationship (Ancestry/23andMe "estimated relationship") is derived from shared-DNA *amount* alone and routinely differs from the truth — the same cM spans several relationships. Carry all candidates, never the single predicted label.
- **A ThruLine as fact — for a different reason.** ThruLines are inferred from *linked member trees* combined with DNA, so they can faithfully propagate *someone else's tree mistake*. The most instructive failure across the cases reviewed is a *false through-line*: Ancestry's ThruLines proposed a father for the target that *looked* DNA-backed but was wrong, and documents (plus the shape of the DNA) disproved it. Per the documented RLP 387 framing, the disproven ThruLine proposed **Hopson Milner** as the father. (The cited source further characterizes this — that the apparent DNA support traced back to the target herself rather than to a distinct ancestor — but that detail is reported by the source and not independently verified.) (Family Locket RLP 387, 2025-12-08)
- **The WATO ranking as a conclusion.** The top hypothesis is the *best next thing to test*, not the answer. Every case reviewed that resolved did so with newspapers, wills, census, or a family bible on top of the DNA. (Kitty Cooper; RLP 387)
- **Public-tree identities.** Two women with the same name and birth year get merged into one person constantly; a single bad merge routes your whole hypothesis to the wrong branch. Verify the intermediate people, don't inherit them. (Kitty Cooper, 2026-01-09)
- **A single record's dates.** Census age drift is normal (one subject read 1815, then 1816, 1817, 1818 across enumerations) — triangulate dates across sources before you let one pin a hypothesis. (Family Locket RLP 387, 2025-12-08)
- **Lean on mid-range matches, not the closest ones.** WATO's discriminating power sits in the mid-range — published as reliable roughly back to a 2nd/3rd-great-grandparent, growing misleading past ~4C1R. A *very close* match is the wrong workhorse for two reasons: (a) it usually **identifies the answer directly** and needs no odds engine, and (b) among several roughly **equidistant** candidate slots it **doesn't discriminate** between them. (Ignore the common backwards rationale that "close ranges overlap" — it's the reverse: total-cM bands overlap worst at *intermediate* degrees like 1C / half-1C / grand-aunt, and even aunt/uncle vs grandparent vs half-sibling are hard to separate by total cM — which is exactly why you carry *all* candidate relationships, never a single label.) (Independence *among* the matches is a separate constraint — see match selection.) (Family Locket, 2025-03-27; DNA Painter WATO docs)

## Match selection dominates the answer (input quality > input volume)

**The right matches matter more than the number of matches** — this is the most repeated lesson across the cases reviewed. (Family Locket, 2024-03-18)

- **Closeness of the question sets the bar.** A closer question needs fewer, closer matches; a distant hypothesis with a weak paper trail needs more matches from *additional independent lines*. A half-sibling match can identify an unknown father in one step; a single first-cousin match often can't, because you still have to exclude the other candidate uncles. (Family Locket, 2024-03-18)
- **Keep the matches independent *of each other*.** DNA Painter's WATO guidance is that the tested descendants be **no more closely related to each other than first cousins** — so each match's DNA is *independent* evidence rather than two views of the same inheritance. (This is a constraint on how the matches relate *among themselves*, not on how close any of them is to the target.) In practice: prefer independent descent lines through **different children** of the ancestral couple — independence is what makes several corroborating matches *genuinely additive*, stronger support rather than one fact counted twice. (It strengthens the case; it is not by itself "proof.") (Family Locket, 2024-03-18; DNA Painter WATO docs)
- **Mind weak, fragmented segments.** False-match risk is driven mainly by **segment-level** quality — the *longest single segment*, SNP density, phasing, and the platform's own matching threshold — not by a flat total-cM number. A single clean ~20 cM segment is generally a *real* match; the thing to distrust is a ~20 cM *total* stitched from several short fragments, each near the noise floor, which can be identical-by-chance. Don't place a match whose total leans on small fragments as if it reliably belongs on a line — use it only as light corroboration of a placement that several stronger, independent matches already support.
- **Grow the pool across databases.** Uploading the tester's raw data to a database that accepts third-party transfers — **GEDmatch** always, **FTDNA** when its transfer program is open (availability comes and goes, so check first) — surfaces matches, and whole *independent descent lines*, absent from the database you started in. (MyHeritage no longer accepts third-party raw-DNA uploads, so a MyHeritage match pool requires testing there; AncestryDNA and 23andMe never accepted uploads.) This is a standard **early** move, and often decisive in burned-county cases where the paper trail is gone.
- **Coverage is real: different relatives inherit different segments.** Testing several relatives of the same generation, and asking key matches to share their *full* match list (Ancestry "Collaborator" access), surfaces relevant matches that don't overlap your own DNA at all. A short research summary + a connection diagram makes matches far likelier to share. (Family Locket, 2026-02-12)
- **Don't over-collect.** Match counts in resolved cases often cluster in the high single digits; more matches from the *wrong* branch resolve nothing. Add matches deliberately to confirm a specific branch, not to bulk up. (Family Locket, 2024-03-18)

## Beyond autosomal: Y-DNA, mtDNA, X-DNA as line-narrowing and exclusion tools

WATO scores **autosomal *total* cM only**. **X-DNA is handled separately** (it follows a distinct, restricted inheritance path), and Y-DNA / mtDNA aren't cM-scored at all. Use these as independent tools *around* the autosomal work — they narrow lines and, powerfully, *exclude* them:

- **Y-DNA** — a strong tool for unknown-**father** cases, but only usable when the target *is* male, or when an eligible male relative on the unbroken paternal line (father, brother, paternal uncle, paternal-line cousin) will test. Y-matches *often* cluster by **surname** and can hand you a candidate surname before you have a tree — but not always: NPEs, adoptions, name changes and pre-surname-era ancestry break the surname signal, so treat a surname cluster as a strong lead, not a guarantee.
- **mtDNA** — traces the **direct maternal** line (mother's mother's mother…). Because mtDNA mutates slowly, a shared maternal ancestor can lie far outside the genealogical timeframe, so an mtDNA **match** is *supportive/consistent-with* a proposed direct-maternal relationship, not confirmation of it. A resolved **mismatch**, however, is a clean *exclusion* of a direct-maternal-line hypothesis.
- **X-DNA** — its restricted inheritance pattern can **exclude** candidate paths (e.g. a father passes no X to a son), narrowing which ancestral line a shared segment can have come from.

## Named failure modes (the least-obvious, most reusable part)

- **Too-broad tree floods the output.** A very large tree or highly varied cM makes WATO generate an unwieldy number of hypotheses (in a documented case it obscured the strongest branch); the fix is *prune, don't add* — focus to the evidence-bearing branch and re-run. (Family Locket, 2025-03-27; a case-specific tactic)
- **The "don't add unknown people as hypotheses" setting** shrinks the search space but can *bias results toward already-known, unmatched children* — know which way you've tilted it. (Family Locket, 2025-03-27)
- **Pedigree collapse** makes one person occupy several tree positions at once; WATO's odds become uninterpretable (same root cause as endogamy inflating cM).
- **The false ThruLine** (above) — a member-tree-inferred hypothesis that looks DNA-backed but points at the wrong parent.
- **Total-cM without triangulation** — placing a match on a line from its total cM alone when the total is really unrelated small segments (see "never trust," above); a *mutually-matching* triangulated segment group is the corroboration.
- **Ambiguous cM bands.** ~269 cM in a documented case sat between "2nd cousin" and "half-1C1R" — never let WATO (or an AI) collapse that to a single relationship; carry *all* candidate relationships forward. (Family Locket, 2025-03-27 — a figure reported by the cited source, not independently verified)
- **Same-name conflation** and **single-record date drift** (above) route hypotheses to the wrong branch before WATO even runs.

## Prompt-craft when an AI is in the loop

1. **Provide complete inputs, don't expect inference** — hand the AI the finished cM/relationship table and the explicit tree structure, not "here's a match list, figure out the tree."
2. **Never ask for a point relationship from a cM number.** Demand *all* candidate relationships with their odds, and route the actual math to WATO / the Shared cM Tool.
3. **Tell it about endogamy / multiple relationships explicitly** — it cannot detect them and cannot quantify the correction; if you don't say so, it will treat inflated cM as a closer relationship.
4. **Flag the weak-segment and total-cM caveats** — tell it that a total leaning on several short fragments isn't reliable placement evidence, and that total cM alone can't distinguish a shared ancestor from coincidental segments. (Placement in WATO comes from a *documented tree connection plus shared cM*; mutually-matching triangulation is useful corroboration when you have segment data, not a WATO prerequisite.)
5. **Prompt-chain the reasoning arc:** cluster → name the ancestral couple → build the descendancy → propose hypotheses. Don't ask for the last step first.
6. **De-identify before you *share data with any third-party tool*, not just before you publish** — WATO, the Shared cM Tool, a cloud LLM, and GEDmatch are all third parties; strip direct identifiers before you paste a match list or upload a kit, and get consent before processing someone else's data (see Ethics). Note raw DNA is inherently re-identifiable, so this is *pseudonymization*, not true anonymization.

## Ethics and the human layer

De-identifying before you use a third-party tool reduces exposure *inside the tool* — but raw DNA is inherently re-identifiable, so it is never true anonymization, and it does not cover the human stakes of the search.

- **Consent, not just de-identification.** Get the tester's informed agreement before working their kit, and be conservative with third parties who never opted into your search — you are processing *their* genetic data too. (Consent is the prudent community norm and, in many jurisdictions, legally required for genetic data — treat it as the default you seek.)
- **NPE sensitivity.** Unknown-parentage work routinely surfaces *not-parent-expected* events (misattributed parentage, undisclosed adoptions, donor conception). These are family secrets with living stakeholders — handle findings privately and let the people involved control disclosure.
- **Making contact is a human act, not a data step.** Identifying a bio-parent or close relative ends the analysis but begins the hardest part: a first approach can be life-changing — welcome or unwelcome — for both sides. Go slowly, lead with the other person's choice to engage, and don't treat a confirmed hypothesis as license to appear on a doorstep.

## Closing the case — and privacy discipline

- **WATO points; DNA testing or documents close.** Treat the top hypothesis as the objective for the next round — which may be recruiting a specific relative to DNA-test *or* a records search: wills that name (or omit) children, marriage and census records, a family bible, newspapers. In the cases reviewed, candidate **elimination** did as much work as the DNA — e.g. ruling out specific Dillard candidates via a will and a family bible (reported by the cited source and not independently verified; the RLP 387 "revisiting" series runs to multiple parts, so treat these specifics as illustrative rather than a complete accounting). (Family Locket RLP 387, 2025-12-08)
- **Work in explicit, revisable phases.** State an objective, test it, and be willing to *reject* it; every case reviewed cycled through wrong candidates before the right one. Being wrong on a hypothesis is the method working, not failing. (Family Locket RLP 387, 2025-12-08)
- **Write it up.** Independent lines and hard-to-document descents need a proof argument, not just a WATO screenshot. (Family Locket, 2024-03-18)
