# Recipe: Correlating evidence & proving identity — the judgment AI gets wrong

## Goal

How an AI assistant (or a genealogist using one) can correlate documentary + DNA evidence and rule on **identity and proof** without inheriting the model's biases — what the human must decide, where the model reliably fails, and the techniques that actually separate same-named people and reconcile conflicts.

## The mental model (proof is one correlated argument, not a pile of facts)

One lens explains most of the modes below — call it **narrative gravity** (this recipe's own shorthand, consistent with known LLM coherence/mode-collapse bias, *not* a term the genealogy sources use). A language model is trained to produce the single tidiest coherent story — and genealogical proof is the *opposite* discipline. Proof is direction-neutral — it splits or unifies identities as the evidence dictates (two men named David, or one Henry assembled from two record sets), keeps conflicts **open** until reconciled, and refuses the neat link. So the model's instincts are systematically inverted from what proof requires: it merges where you must separate, votes where you must weigh, and states an answer where you must build an argument. (Two modes below — skipped impossibility checks and dismissed negative evidence — are as much plain omission as coherence-seeking; the lens is a mnemonic for the pattern, not a claim of one literal cause.)

Two consequences follow, and they are the whole recipe: (1) treat the model as a fast assistant that **assembles the dossier and drafts the prose**, never as the authority that **rules** on identity or proof (the sibling's "every AI output is a hypothesis to verify" frame applies wholesale here). (2) Proof is **one correlated argument** — DNA and documents converging on a single hypothesis, conflicts explained, negatives ruled out — not a stack of facts the model can total.

## Hard rules — apply these before you rule (the load-bearing directives)

1. **Stop expanding a questionable branch and gather every source first** — before any conclusion, before any merge, before any DNA argument.
2. **No identity claim until the descriptor dossier exists — and sort the table BY descriptor, not by name.** A distinct verified signature (or any strong descriptor) can support a split, a matching one a union — but only corroborated by the rest of the dossier, never as a lone identity test.
3. **Any unresolved biological or chronological impossibility BLOCKS the merge** — however smooth the prose reads. A genuine impossibility (childbearing window, post-mortem fatherhood) separates; a softer anomaly (a duplicate census enumeration, a household member who appears then vanishes) is a CONFLICT to investigate, not an automatic separator.
4. **Keep same-name candidates in SEPARATE research projects** so their records never cross-contaminate.
5. **Weigh sources, don't count them.** Five online trees copying one claim aren't five witnesses — they're one unverified claim repeated. Classify each *statement* by whether its informant had firsthand knowledge of THAT fact (a death certificate's informant usually knew the death firsthand, the birth only secondhand) — never stamp a whole record "primary" or "secondary."
6. **A record's mere mention of a person doesn't prove a relationship it never asserts** — but an unstated relationship can still be proved by an explicit, written correlation of indirect evidence (never by assertion). A parent's signature on a standard-form marriage bond does **not** prove a minor bride.
7. **"No record found" is a search result, not evidence** — and absence of a death record is not evidence of life. **Log every search with its date** (online coverage changes over time).
8. **An indirect conclusion must be WRITTEN as an explicit correlation argument**, never asserted as if direct.
9. **Document each DNA test-taker's parent-child descent to the common ancestor BEFORE the match counts** — then require paper descent and genetic sharing to converge on **one** hypothesis, and judge match **quality**, not count.
10. **On a collaborative tree, read the change log bottom-up** to recover the profile's original intended identity before untangling.
11. **You rule on identity and proof. The model assembles and drafts — it never concludes.**

## What the HUMAN must own (never delegate to an AI conclusion)

- **Framing the question and the standard of proof.** Decide upfront exactly what you're proving (an identity? a specific parent-child link? an event?) and what would COUNT as proof — a reasoned probabilistic argument, not certainty. A wrong or vague frame upstream invalidates everything below it, and the model will happily "prove" a question you never meant to ask.
- **The merge-vs-separate ruling on same-named people.** The AI can build the descriptor dossier; *you* decide whether the records describe one person or two. A wrong merge cascades — false relationships, false record hints, combined families across generations — and reshapes the whole tree above it. Separating is the harder call and runs against the model's reduce-entities instinct. (Kathryn Grant; Jan Joyce, RLP 399)
- **The reconciliation of conflicting evidence.** Assign weight by informant proximity and source *independence*, per **statement** (records are hybrid), and articulate *why* the sources disagree. Correlation is reasoning about reliability, not tallying — the model's "pick the popular value" default is exactly the error to override. (Power of Proof; Slagle)
- **The sufficiency judgment on negative evidence.** Decide when a search was exhaustive enough — record loss, incomplete coverage, indexing error all ruled out — that an absence now carries evidentiary weight. It hinges on jurisdiction and collection coverage the model can gather and compare, but only the human can verify it and make the sufficiency judgment; both over- and under-claiming are cheap for a model to state confidently. (RLP 409)
- **The correlation of DNA and documents into a single argument.** When a proof relies on DNA it must be integrated INTO the documentary argument, not run beside it — the model instead hands you two independent verdicts ("DNA suggests X; records suggest Y"). An undocumented descent path means a match can't yet be attributed to the proposed common ancestor; and a sufficiently documented conclusion can meet the standard without DNA at all. (Why Document DNA Links; Coverage)
- **The final evidentiary ruling.** "Can I explain, source, and defend this in the real historical record?" The model drafts the proof summary; the human rules on whether the standard is actually met. Proof validation is professional responsibility, not a delegable output. (Denyse Allen, Lesson 5; How to Know if You've Met the GPS)
- **The discipline to stop and verify *before* extending.** Treat each source as (a) belonging to the correct person and (b) actually proving the claimed kinship — and **stop tree expansion on a questionable branch to gather every source first.** The model's default is to extend the coherent narrative; you impose "wrong until proven right." (Wrong Until Proven Right; Jan Joyce, RLP 399)

## Where AI reliably goes wrong (the named failure modes — never trust)

- **Same-name collapse (entity-merging bias).** Given two record sets sharing a name and rough place/era, the model defaults to ONE person and stitches a single coherent timeline. It treats a shared name as identity evidence when a name is the **weakest** discriminator (a FamilySearch example counts **hundreds of thousands of "John Smith"** profiles). Tell: it fuses "Maryland Henry" with "Ohio Henry," or absorbs two same-named wives into one profile, without ever demanding the discriminating descriptors. Corrective: no identity claim until the descriptor dossier exists. (Kathryn Grant, Same Name Trap; Jan Joyce, RLP 399; Dewitt case; One Man, Two Same-Named Wives)
- **Skips the reality-check filters.** It won't run the biological/chronological impossibility tests that expose a bad merge — a woman born in the 1800s "bearing" children recorded in the 1600s, a man "fathering" a child ten years after his death, or a **duplicate enumeration** in one census year it never pauses to explain. It accepts an impossible merged timeline as long as the prose reads smoothly. (Kathryn Grant, Same Name Trap & Recognizing Problems; Seventeen Rules)
- **Conflict-flattening (vote-counting instead of reconciliation).** Faced with conflicting evidence, the model picks the majority / most-recent / most-official-**sounding** value and states one answer, instead of weighting each **statement** by informant proximity and explaining *why* they differ. It counts sources rather than judging independence — **five online trees copying one claim aren't five witnesses; they're one unverified claim repeated** — and treats a derivative/secondary statement as equal to a primary one. (Power of Proof; Slagle; Ann Tanner)
- **Over-reading a document beyond what it states.** It infers a relationship or status the record never asserts — reading a father's signature on a **standard-form marriage bond** as proof of an underage bride, or treating any record that merely *mentions* a person as proof of the claimed parentage. Rule: **a record that merely mentions a person doesn't prove a parentage it never states** — though that parentage may still be provable by correlating indirect evidence. (Wrong Until Proven Right — Josiah How; Slagle)
- **Negative-evidence errors in BOTH directions.** It conflates a negative *search result* with negative *evidence*. It over-claims — "no death record found, therefore he was still alive" (**absence of a death record is not evidence of life**) — or dismisses a meaningful absence outright. It won't do the ruling-out work that converts absence into evidence: corroborating searches, excluding record loss / incomplete coverage / indexing error, and confirming the record **should** exist. (Negative evidence is the hardest type to apply.) (RLP 409; Writing Up Negative Findings; Seventeen Rules; Lesson 5)
- **Indirect-evidence over-confidence.** It asserts an indirect conclusion as if it were direct, skipping the explicit correlation argument indirect evidence *requires* — chaining multiple clues into a reasoned case. It states the answer instead of building the argument that would support it. (RLP 409; Lesson 5; Wrong Until Proven Right)
- **DNA/documentary siloing (parallel-track fallacy).** Asked to weigh genetic and paper evidence, the model returns **two separate verdicts** ("DNA suggests X; records suggest Y") instead of one proof argument. It uses matches whose parent-child descent to the common ancestor is **undocumented** (so they can't yet function as evidence), ignores that an unphased autosomal match doesn't by itself reveal which **side** (that needs phasing, tested relatives, or documentary framing — routed to ai-for-dna-research), and equates "more matches" with "stronger proof" when match **quality** plus the strength of the **non-DNA** evidence is what resolves the question. (Why Document DNA Links; How Many Matches; Using DNA Source Citations; Coverage)
- **Narrative-gravity / crowd-tree credulity (the meta-failure).** Trained for the tidiest story, the model resolves ambiguity toward **fewer entities** and accepts other users' trees, record hints, or a same-named **"gateway to royalty"** link as fact — the very errors that propagate across online trees and DNA matches (an incorrect merge then *generates* false record hints). It won't adopt "wrong until proven right" or "stop expanding the branch and gather every source first" unless explicitly told. (Jan Joyce, RLP 399; Gateway Ancestors; Wrong Until Proven Right; RLP 391; New Merge Experience)

- **De-novo fabrication (inventing the bridging fact).** Distinct from crowd-tree credulity (which propagates *others'* errors), this is the model **inventing** a record, citation, date, or relationship that never existed to complete the coherent story — the sharpest form of narrative gravity and the most dangerous mode in proof work, because a fabricated source *is* fake evidence. Never accept a record or citation the model produced without confirming it exists in the actual repository. (Verifying any AI-surfaced record/citation is detailed in **ai-for-documentary-records**.)
- **Sycophancy / anchoring on the answer you WANT.** Shown which ancestor you hope to prove — the famous, royal, or brick-wall-breaking one — the model bends the argument toward it, over-weighting confirming evidence and skipping the disconfirming search. Distinct from crowd-tree credulity: it defers to the *operator's* wish, not other users' trees. Frame the question neutrally and demand the disconfirming case ("argue this is the WRONG person") before accepting a link.

## Techniques that work

- **Identity dossier / one-row-per-record descriptor table — and sort it BY descriptor.** Stop expanding the branch, then capture **every** descriptor per record in its own column — the Dewitt case used **40+ columns**: dates, places, occupations, spouses, children, neighbors, signatures, and behavioral **"psychographics"** (the case label "Bad Barney"). The core move is the **sort:** a distinct signature (or cluster of descriptors) surfaces separate people, a matching one unifies records a name alone couldn't — corroborated across the dossier, since a signature can be clerk-written or vary. Have the AI *build* the table; you *read the sort*. (Jan Joyce, RLP 399; From Chaos to Clarity)
- **Three-bucket identity triage.** Same-name+same-place / same-name+different-place / different-names — each needs a different separation tactic. **Parental sets** and the **FAN** network (Friends, Associates, Neighbors — Elizabeth Shown Mills' term) discriminate same-named people far better than name-spelling variants; for common names, keep candidates in **separate research projects** so records don't cross-contaminate. (Jan Joyce, RLP 399; One Man, Two Same-Named Wives; RLP 288; Seventeen Rules)
- **Reality-check filters on any merged or proposed-merge timeline.** A genuine biological/chronological impossibility (childbearing window, post-mortem fatherhood) **blocks the merge**; softer anomalies (a duplicate census enumeration, spouses/children that appear then vanish) are **conflicts to investigate** — resolve them, don't merge over them, but don't treat them as automatic separators either.** On a collaborative tree, read the **change log bottom-up** to recover the profile's *original intended identity* before untangling. (Kathryn Grant, Same Name Trap & Recognizing Problems; New Merge Experience)
- **Conflict resolution by reliability, not by vote.** Judge each **statement** by its informant's basis of knowledge (a death certificate's informant usually knew the death firsthand but the birth secondhand — classify the statement, not the record), weight by informant proximity, count only **independent** information streams (copies aren't corroboration), correct for document conventions (a standard-form bond signed by a parent doesn't prove minority), and **explain the conflict's cause before choosing a value.** (Power of Proof; Ann Tanner; Slagle)
- **Negative-evidence gate.** A negative finding carries weight only when the source system and circumstances create a **justified expectation** that the missing information would appear — after ruling out record loss, incomplete coverage, and indexing error, and confirming the record **should** exist. Multiple failed searches alone never convert it (that is just vote-counting). **Log every search's date** — online coverage changes over time. (RLP 409; Writing Up Negative Findings)
- **Indirect-evidence discipline.** Never let "a record mentions X" stand in for "a record proves the relationship." An indirect conclusion must be **written as an explicit correlation argument** chaining the clues, not asserted. (Wrong Until Proven Right; RLP 409; Lesson 5)
- **DNA + paper as one proof.** Document each test-taker's parent-child chain to the common ancestor **before** using the match as evidence; require the documented descent path and the genetic sharing to **converge on the same hypothesis** before the match counts as proof. (Match selection, clustering, coverage across independent descendant lines, and triangulation belong to **unknown-parentage-wato** — route them there, don't re-derive them here.) (Why Document DNA Links; How Many Matches; Coverage; Using DNA Source Citations; RLP 409)

## Worked mini-examples (the reasoning logic)

- **Two-way descriptor sort splits and unifies at once (Dewitt).** Sorting a 40+-column spreadsheet of "David Dewitt" *and* "Henry Dewitt" records simultaneously **split** two men named David (two distinct signatures on an 1833 petition) **and unified** "Maryland Henry" with "Ohio Henry" — Maryland Henry vanished from Maryland tax records in 1815–1816, Ohio Henry bought Holmes County land on 26 May 1815 — as one man, David's father. The name carried zero discriminating power; the descriptor pattern carried all of it. (From Chaos to Clarity)
- **Overlapping simultaneous locations are a HARD separator (Scandinavian).** Tax records placing one Goodman Rasmusson on farm A in parish X **and** another on farm B in parish Y at the *same time* prove two families, not one man with two marriages — simultaneity settles it before any softer evidence is weighed. Only then did indirect evidence (naming patterns, godparents) pick the Rasmus born 1720 as the man who died 1773, stated as **"more likely," not certain** (correct genealogical calibration). (RLP 286)
- **Reconcile, don't average (Slagle birth date).** Census enumerations implying birth years scattered across **1830–1835** (1850 → 1835; 1860/1880/1900 → 1834), plus a marriage bond, death certificate, and headstone (**4 May 1834**), were reconciled to **4 May 1834** by weighting informant proximity and geographic evidence — and the father's signature on the **$200 standard-form marriage bond was correctly *not* read as proof the bride was a minor.** Pre-1940 census ages carry an unknown informant; **1940 is the first U.S. census to mark who reported.** (When Birthdates Don't Agree)
- **Resolve a same-name fork by tracing each candidate *forward* (457-person tree).** An entire 457-person tree rested on **one unverified parent-child link**; two same-name census candidates were eliminated by following each one's *later* residences, marriages, and families until only one stayed consistent. One bad link invalidated everything above it. (Genealogical Proof Standard | Ancestry)
- **Absence into evidence (Henderson Weatherford / Eliza Poling).** Repeated failed searches became negative *evidence* only after corroboration and ruling out record loss / coverage / indexing; a **missing 1888 death registration** counted because the person was known to be in the area and should have been recorded. (RLP 409; Writing Up Negative Findings)
- **Undocumented descent blocks the DNA argument.** Two starting test-takers lacked documented lines of descent, so their matches couldn't serve as strong evidence until the **paper descent was built** — proof came from correlating the DNA cluster *with* a documented line to the common ancestor, not from the match list alone. (Why Document DNA Links; RLP 409)
- **"Wrong until proven right" (Josiah How).** Census, military, and pension records *about* Josiah didn't prove the family story until each was checked for belonging to the correct man **and** actually proving kinship — which overturned the story (he survived the Civil War). (Wrong Until Proven Right)
- **Immigrant identity across two countries (Ane Jensen).** Connecting the right person in the US with the right person in the home country was itself an indirect-evidence correlation problem — resolved by correlating census and newspaper conflicts, *not* by assuming the matching name was the match. (How to Know if You've Met the GPS)

## Pitfalls worth internalizing (the least-obvious, most-LLM-usable part)

- It will merge on a name match and present the fused timeline as fact — even when the timeline is biologically impossible.
- It counts sources instead of weighing them; copied trees inflate a single error into false "corroboration."
- It reads more into a document than the document says (a bond, a mention → a proven relationship).
- It flips "no record found" into either "so the fact is true" or "so it doesn't matter" — skipping the exhaustiveness work.
- It states indirect conclusions as direct ones, with no written correlation argument.
- It hands you DNA and paper as two verdicts and calls "more matches" stronger proof.
- It trusts crowd trees, record hints, and gateway-to-royalty links — and a merge it accepts *spawns* new false hints.

## Verification discipline

- **Run the loop, in order: stop → dossier → reality-check → reconcile → converge → rule.** Don't extend a branch past a link you haven't separated, reconciled, and (where DNA is involved) documented on both the paper and genetic sides.
- **Make the model show its work, then audit it.** Require the descriptor sort, the per-statement reliability weighting, the negative-search log (with dates), and the written indirect-evidence argument — a conclusion with none of these is a hypothesis, not proof.
- **Rule on identity and proof yourself.** The AI drafts the summary; you decide whether it can be explained, sourced, and defended in the historical record. (Lesson 5; How to Know if You've Met the GPS)
- **Scope US/Anglo-specific facts before applying them.** "Absence of a death record is not evidence of life," "1940 is the first U.S. census to mark the informant," and marriage-bond conventions are jurisdiction-specific — the *reasoning* transfers, but the concrete record facts must be re-verified against the relevant country's record system.

Endogamy appears here only as a reason DNA can mislead, so corroborate with documents. De-identify living individuals and get consent before pasting DNA/match data into any cloud tool (per the sibling — not re-derived here).
